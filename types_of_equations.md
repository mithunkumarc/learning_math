Mathematical equations are classified based on the nature of their variables, exponents, and operations. They range from simple relationships where variables have a power of 1 to complex differential systems that describe how things change over time. [1, 2, 3, 4, 5] 
## Algebraic Equations
Algebraic equations involve variables, coefficients, and standard arithmetic operations where the exponents of the variables are integers. [6, 7, 8, 9] 

* Linear Equations: The highest exponent of the variable is 1, forming a straight line when graphed.
* Standard form: $ax + b = 0$ [10, 11] 
* Quadratic Equations: The highest exponent of the variable is 2, creating a parabola (U-shaped curve) on a graph.
* Standard form: $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$ [12, 13, 14, 15, 16] 
* Polynomial Equations: Equations featuring variables raised to any non-negative integer power, such as cubic (degree 3) or quartic (degree 4) equations.
* Standard form: $a_n x^n + a_{n-1} x^{n-1} + \dots + a_0 = 0$ [17, 18, 19, 20, 21] 
* Rational Equations: Equations that contain one or more rational expressions, meaning the variable appears in the denominator of a fraction.
* Example: $\frac{1}{x} + \frac{2}{x+1} = 3$ [22, 23, 24, 25] 
* Radical Equations: Equations where the variable is trapped inside a radical symbol, such as a square root or cube root.
* Example: $\sqrt{x + 5} = 4$ [26, 27, 28, 29] 

## Transcendental Equations
Transcendental equations go beyond standard algebra and cannot be solved using only a finite sequence of algebraic operations. [30, 31, 32] 

* Exponential Equations: Equations where the variable appears as an exponent or power.
* Example: $a^x = b$ [33, 34] 
* Logarithmic Equations: Equations that involve the logarithm of an expression containing a variable.
* Example: $\log_b(x) = c$ [35] 
* Trigonometric Equations: Equations involving trigonometric functions like sine, cosine, or tangent acting on a variable.
* Example: $\sin(x) + \cos(x) = 1$ [36, 37, 38, 39, 40] 

## Calculus and Dynamic Equations
These equations focus on rates of change and accumulation, essential for modeling physics, engineering, and fluid dynamics. [41, 42] 

* Differential Equations: Equations that contain a function and its derivatives (the rates at which the function changes).
* Ordinary Differential Equation (ODE): Involves functions of a single variable, like $\frac{dy}{dx} = 3x$.
   * Partial Differential Equation (PDE): Involves functions of multiple variables and their partial derivatives, like $\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial t^2} = c^2 \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}$. [43, 44, 45, 46, 47] 
* Integral Equations: Equations where an unknown function appears under an integral sign.
* Example: $f(x) = \int_a^x g(t) dt$ [48]
